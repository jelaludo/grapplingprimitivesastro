A wedge is a structural insertion that creates or preserves space, disrupts alignment, or amplifies force. In grappling, wedges are anatomical tools—elbows, knees, feet, hands—placed between the opponent and the ground or between limbs to create mechanical advantage.

🔩 Core Functions:
Disrupt Structure – Break posture, frames, or base

Create Leverage – Serve as pivot points for sweeps or submissions

Maintain Space – Prevent opponent from collapsing into dominant position

Transfer Force – Direct energy into weak structural angles (e.g., armpit, hip crease)

🦴 Biomechanical Reality:
A wedge is effective when it's:

Inserted deep (near fulcrum)

Structurally aligned (bones stacked, tension braced)

Timed under pressure (before collapse or counter)

Elbow inside knee = wedge for guard recovery

Knee in armpit = wedge for omoplata or shoulder control

Foot on hip = wedge for distance and framing

Shoulder under chin = wedge for passing or neck control

🧠 Strategic Use:
Bottom Game: Wedges buy space and time

Top Game: Wedges isolate limbs, elevate hips, and dismantle defense

Transitions: Wedges control tempo—insert to slow them, remove to accelerate you

🔁 Principle:
Grappling without wedges is pushing a door with no hinge.
Insert wedge → load structure → rotate or collapse opponent.

Summary:
Wedges are grappling’s invisible levers. They convert pressure into posture breaks, space into control, and friction into force.
