analogous to proprioception, but directed outward to the opponent. Afaik Coined by me Jelaludo.  

Alioception: the embodied sense of an opponent’s position, movement, and balance state through direct contact—enabling reactive and anticipatory adjustments in grappling.

From Latin alius (“other”) + -ception (“perception”)

The embodied capacity to detect, interpret, and respond to the position, movement, tension, balance, and intention of another person's body through direct physical contact. Unlike proprioception, which governs awareness of one’s own body, alioception operates outwardly—enabling real-time adaptation to external forces, subtle shifts in pressure, and structural vulnerabilities in an opponent.

In grappling, alioception underpins timing, sensitivity, and control. It allows practitioners to "feel" openings before they appear visually, to preempt resistance, and to maintain dynamic connection without overcommitting. It blends tactile input, kinesthetic intuition, and neuromuscular calibration into an opponent-centered perceptual system.

In short: alioception is the trained sense of the other’s body, through your own.

I see a similarity with Listening Skills in Taichi.

Listening Skill – 聴勁 / tīng jìn
Literal meaning: “Listening energy” or “Listening force”

Definition: The refined ability to perceive the opponent’s intention, balance, structure, and movement through touch—without relying on sight.

Practiced through constant physical contact (sticky hands, push hands), it trains the practitioner to feel shifts in pressure, alignment, relaxation, or tension.

It’s not passive—it's an active, receptive awareness, like a radar tuned to another's body dynamics.

Ting jin emphasizes intent and energy; alioception leans into biomechanics and contact strategy, but they overlap in function.

Both are trainable through constant-contact drills, sparring, and tactile sensitivity work.

Both reject reliance on visual cues, favoring a deep embodied reading of the opponent.
