'*Efficient kinetic chains boost sports output by optimizing energy transfer, such as in baseball pitching where lower-body force contributes up to 50% of throw power. Skilled athletes show greater trunk rotation and core stability, leading to higher ball velocity in soccer kicks or tennis serves. Disruptions reduce velocity and force at the endpoint*.'

'*Chain "breakage"—like weak hips or poor core control—forces distal joints (shoulder, elbow) to compensate, raising overload risk. Repetitive sports thicken fascia and create imbalances, increasing injury profiles in overhead activities. Holistic assessments targeting the full chain cut injury rates and enhance performance*.'
[Role of kinetic chain in sports performance and injury risk: a narrative review - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10893580/)

Every powerful movement in grappling follows the same pattern: force starts at the ground, travels through the feet, up the legs, through the hips and core, and out to the limbs. This is the kinetic chain.

A bridge is more than a back exercise; it's an attempt to create a ground-reaction-force where the feet push into the mat, the body is connected, and the hips express that force upward. A good sweep doesn't start with the arms pulling; it starts with hip displacement that the arms merely directing. When the chain is connected (skeleton aligned, core engaged, force flowing through structure rather than muscles), you feel heavy and efficient. When it's broken (loose core, bent spine, arms working alone), you feel weak and get tired fast.

Many old martial arts saying refer to being 'rooted', similar ideas. Every time a technique feels like it takes too much effort, check the chain. Somewhere between the ground and your point of contact, there's a leak. Find it, reconnect it, and it should get easier.
