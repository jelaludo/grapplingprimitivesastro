"自分を作れ" ("build yourself", as an imperative. Jibun wo Tsukure) represents my reframing of how we typically understand what makes technique work in grappling. The conventional teaching model, inherited from Kodokan Judo's Kuzushi → Tsukuri → Kake sequence, is typically rendered as "off-balance your opponent, then set up your throw, then execute." This places the opponent as the primary object of attention from the very first step. But buried inside the concept of Tsukuri (作り, "making/preparation") is a quieter, more foundational instruction from Kano Jigoro (founder of Judo): 自分を作る, i.e. to make yourself, to organize your own body first. This project's core argument is that this half of Tsukuri is actually the prerequisite for everything else. You cannot reliably disrupt the opponent's structure if your own is not in order, and once you understand how to stack and build your body, it helps you understand the opponent's structure as well.

This matters because what separates an experienced grappler from a beginner isn't a larger catalog of techniques, it's an invisible quality of body. Some have called it "invisible jiu-jitsu", I posit that "self-organization" of the body is what leads to that quality. The experienced person feels heavy, connected, hard to move, harder still to prevent from moving. Their tension is efficient (concentrated through the core rather than parasitically engaging at the extremities or firing unnecessary muscles), their kinetic chains are linked through the center of mass, their skeletal alignment stacks load-bearing parts so that force flows cleanly to the ground and between limbs. These aren't conscious decisions in the moment; they're trained defaults. The four visualization panels (1. tension economy, 2. internal connections, 3. ground path, and 4. stacking alignment) are an attempt to make this invisible architecture visible, to show what a body "feels like" when it has been "built" in that old Judo sense.

The deeper implication, and the reason I try to reframe the classical sequence as Set → Break → Execute rather than Break → Set → Execute, is pedagogical. If you teach students that grappling begins with disrupting the opponent, they chase the other person's body and neglect their own. If instead you teach that grappling begins with organizing yourself, i.e. achieving structural integrity, efficient tension, clear alignment, etc. Then disruption of the opponent becomes a downstream consequence of that self-organization meeting their disorganization. Grappling can be seen as the management of two structures, and you can only manage the interaction between two structures if you've first learned to manage one: your own.
