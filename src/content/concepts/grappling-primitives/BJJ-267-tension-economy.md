An experienced grappler feels heavy, connected, hard to move. A beginner doing the same technique feels stiff and burnable. The difference is tension economy.

The experienced person engages only what's needed: core tension for structure, grip tension for control, everything else relaxed. The beginner tenses everything. Biceps, neck, jaw, shoulders, burning energy on muscles that aren't contributing anything.

Parasitic tension (unnecessary muscle firing) does three bad things: it wastes cardio, it reduces your ability to feel what the opponent is doing, and it creates rigidity that a skilled opponent can exploit. Stiff arms get kimura'd. Tense necks get choked. Rigid postures get swept.

The fix isn't "just relax." It's learning which muscles actually need to fire for each position and deliberately letting everything else go quiet. In side control top, your hips and core do the work while your arms stay loose and reactive. In closed guard, your legs grip while your upper body stays soft enough to feel their weight shifts.

This takes years, but it's one of the biggest separators between belts.
