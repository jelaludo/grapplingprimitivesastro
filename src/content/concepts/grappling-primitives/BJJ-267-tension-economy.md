An experienced grappler feels heavy, connected, hard to move. A beginner doing the same technique feels stiff and easily movable. Part of the difference is 'tension economy', e.g. how the tension is distributed, how many unnecessary muscles is the beginner firing?

The experienced person engages only what's needed: core tension for structure, grip tension for control (typically not using all five fingers), everything else not required for the goal at hand should be relaxed. The beginner tenses everything. Biceps, neck, jaw, shoulders, every fingers in a death grip, etc.  burning energy on muscles that aren't contributing anything.

'Parasitic tension' (unnecessary muscle firing) does three bad things: it wastes cardio, it reduces your ability to feel what the opponent is doing, and it creates rigidity that a skilled opponent can exploit. Stiff arms get kimura'd. Tense necks get choked. Rigid postures get swept.  

One of my greatest influences in Martial Arts used to call it 無駄を省く, 'get rid of the useless'.

The fix isn't "just relax." It's learning which muscles actually need to fire for each position and deliberately letting everything else go quiet. In side control top, your hips and core do the work while your arms stay loose and reactive to counter theirs. In closed guard, your legs grip just enough to connect and stay close while your upper body stays soft enough to feel their weight shifts.

This takes years, but it's one of the biggest separators between belts.
