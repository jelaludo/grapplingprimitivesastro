Torque is rotational force around a pivot point, and it shows up everywhere in grappling. The formula is straightforward: force times distance from the pivot. That means small changes in where you grip can make a massive difference in how much rotational pressure you create.

A kimura is pure torque on the shoulder joint. A heel hook applies torque to the knee. Even something like breaking someone's posture in closed guard involves torque around their hips. The further out you grip on their arm or leg, the more rotational force you get for the same effort.

This is why experienced grapplers are so particular about grip placement. Sliding your grip two inches further down someone's forearm during a kimura doesn't sound like much, but it meaningfully increases the torque on their shoulder. Details matter.
