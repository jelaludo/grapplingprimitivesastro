Start from the end. Take a fully extended armbar, locked in, your partner's arm is out. Your only job is to finish from there. Do that until it's automatic, until you don't even think about it. Then rewind one step: now the arm is almost extended but they still have a grip. Break the grip and finish. Drill that. Then one more step back: you have the arm isolated but haven't started the extension yet. Then further back: you've just secured the position but haven't isolated anything.

Each step backward adds one more problem to solve, but **you already own everything downstream**. By the time you've rewound to the initial entry, you've built the entire chain from finish to start, and every link in it is solid.  As long as you find an entry point, you're confident that you can finish.  

I learned this from Wim, and I think that this confidence makes for BETTER TRAINING PARTNERS, because they have control they don't need to rip anything violently to achieve their goals.

This is the opposite of how most people train, which is entry first, hope for the best at the end. End-game training builds confidence in the kill zone first.
