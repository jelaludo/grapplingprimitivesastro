King Mithridates VI built immunity to poison by taking small doses daily, gradually increasing the amount until lethal doses couldn't touch him. It is still a modern concept of medicine, within limits! (*The fundamental principle of controlled, gradual exposure is utilized in several areas of modern medicine, representing a scientifically controlled form of mithradatism*).

Defensive grappling works similarly.

You don't learn to escape back control by avoiding it. You learn by putting yourself there deliberately, at low intensity, with a cooperative partner, and working the problem. Then you increase resistance. Then you try it against tougher opponents. Then in competition.

The two non-negotiables: wilful exposure (*you choose to be in the bad spot*) and graduated dosing (*start cooperative, build to live, increase intensity*). Skipping the gradual part leads to getting smashed and learning nothing.  Or getting hurt.  Skipping the exposure part means you're avoiding the positions you most need to practice.  I see Garry Tonon as the embodiment of that philosophy, but I'm sure there are many others.

This applies to every scary position. Mount escapes, leg lock defense (Get Off My Legs! Gringo!), back survival, etc. Start with a partner who lets you work, add resistance in steps, and eventually pressure-test it against the killers in your gym. That's how comfort in bad positions is actually built.

It dovetails with the concept of end-game training*, whereby you START with, say, a fully extended armbar, and try to finish from there, and once you've developed confidence finishing from there, rewind to one step before the full extension and try again, then one more step back, etc.
